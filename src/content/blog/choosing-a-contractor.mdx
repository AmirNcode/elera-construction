You wouldn't hire a surgeon who couldn't tell you which hospital they operate in. But homeowners regularly sign $100,000+ renovation contracts after a single phone call and one walk-through, with a contractor whose last name they barely caught.

Renovation horror stories aren't usually about bad people. They're about mismatches: a homeowner who needed a project manager hired a handyman; a contractor who quoted by gut got blindsided by hidden costs and rage-quit the project. The way to avoid the mismatch is to ask specific questions. These seven separate the professionals from the rest.

## 1. "Can I see your $2M general liability insurance certificate?"

Real answer: they email it to you within an hour, with the policy number, broker name, and effective dates clearly visible. Or they show it on their phone right at the consultation.

Bad answer: "We're licensed and insured" with no document. "I'll send it later." Vague reassurances.

This matters because if a worker is injured on your property and the contractor isn't actually insured, you can become personally liable. $2M GL is the bar for serious residential renovation in the GTA.

## 2. "Are you WSIB-cleared?"

Real answer: yes, with a clearance certificate they can produce. WSIB (Workplace Safety & Insurance Board) covers employees of the contractor if they're hurt on the job. Without it, you might be on the hook.

You can verify any contractor's WSIB status yourself at [www.wsib.ca](https://www.wsib.ca) using their business name or number.

## 3. "Can you give me three references from completed jobs in the last 12 months?"

Real answer: names, neighborhoods, phone numbers or emails, project descriptions. You'll need to actually call or email two or three of them.

Bad answer: "We have lots of references but we don't share contact info." Photos only, no contacts.

When you call references, ask: "Were they on budget?" "How did they handle change orders?" "Would you hire them again?"

## 4. "Can you walk me through your written quote?"

Real answer: a line-item proposal — labour separated from materials, each major scope item broken out, allowances called out explicitly. Payment milestones tied to construction verifiables ("after framing inspection passes") not calendar dates.

Bad answer: a one-line lump sum. "Kitchen renovation — $80,000." A lump sum hides everything — you can't compare quotes, you can't track change orders, and disputes are inevitable.

## 5. "What's your change-order procedure?"

Real answer: any scope change is captured in a written change order signed by both parties before work begins. Cost, schedule impact, and reason are documented. No verbal "while you're at it" changes.

Bad answer: "We figure it out as we go." This is the #1 source of renovation horror stories.

## 6. "What's your warranty?"

Real answer: a written warranty (typically 1–2 years on workmanship for residential renovation), with what's covered and what's excluded clearly stated. The contractor is still in business in two years to honour it.

Bad answer: "We stand behind our work" with no document. Verbal warranties evaporate when something fails.

## 7. "Who is my point of contact day-to-day, and how often will I hear from them?"

Real answer: one project lead, by name, with weekly updates by text/email/in-person walkthrough. You know who's coming, what's happening that day, and what's coming next week.

Bad answer: "Call the office if you need anything." This means there's no project management — you'll be chasing trades and answering questions you shouldn't be answering.

## What if a contractor refuses to answer these?

That's your answer. A serious contractor will be relieved to be asked — these are the questions that separate them from competitors. A contractor who deflects, gets defensive, or claims the questions are "unusual" is showing you something important.

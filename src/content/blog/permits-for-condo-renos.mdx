Renovating a condo in the GTA looks easy until you discover the work needs board approval, an electrician licensed for the building's electrical scheme, an acoustic underlayment certified to a specific STC rating, a status certificate, and a building permit you didn't realize applied. Then it gets expensive and slow.

Here's the order to do it in.

## Before you do anything — call the property manager

Before drawings, before quotes, before deciding what you're renovating: call your building's property manager and ask for:

1. The current declaration and rules document (the parts about renovations).
2. The renovation request form (most buildings have one).
3. The list of required contractor documentation (usually $2M+ GL insurance, WSIB clearance, and possibly a security deposit).
4. The hours work is permitted (often 9am–5pm weekdays, no weekends).
5. The acoustic-underlayment STC/IIC requirement if you're touching flooring.
6. The elevator booking procedure for material delivery and disposal.

Some of these documents take weeks to obtain. Start here.

## City of Toronto permits: when you need them

City permits cover **structural, plumbing, electrical, and HVAC changes that affect anything beyond your unit's interior walls**. In a condo, common triggers:

- **Plumbing rough-in changes.** Moving a sink, toilet, shower, or dishwasher more than a few inches.
- **New plumbing fixtures.** Adding a second bathroom or a wet bar.
- **Structural changes.** Almost never relevant in a condo — load-bearing walls are usually outside your unit boundary.
- **Electrical panel changes.** Adding circuits beyond what the unit panel allows.
- **HVAC duct or fan changes.** New range hood exhaust to the building's riser is sometimes a permit issue.

What you usually **don't** need a city permit for:

- Painting, tile, cabinetry, countertops, flooring (within your unit) — no plumbing/electrical changes.
- Replacing existing fixtures (toilet, sink, shower head) in their existing locations.
- New light fixtures wired to existing circuits.

When in doubt, ask the City of Toronto's building department — they answer the phone.

## Condo board approval: almost always required

Even if the city doesn't need a permit, the condo board usually does. Boards approve based on the renovation rules in the declaration — which typically require:

- Contractor insurance certificates.
- Schedule of work (start date, end date).
- Description of the scope.
- Acoustic underlayment spec for flooring (typically IIC 50/STC 50 or higher).
- Sometimes a refundable security deposit ($500–$2,000) against damage to common elements.

Board approval can take 2–6 weeks. Submit early.

## The status certificate gotcha

If you're **buying** a condo with the intent to renovate, request a **status certificate** during your offer's conditional period. The status certificate includes the renovation rules — what's allowed, what's banned. Some buildings prohibit certain flooring types entirely, ban tankless water heaters in units, or require specific contractors for plumbing/electrical work.

We've seen homeowners discover after closing that they can't install the engineered hardwood they wanted because the building requires a sound rating their chosen product doesn't meet. The fix was easy — but it required a different product at a higher cost.

## Hours of work

Most GTA condo buildings allow work 9am–5pm weekdays only. No weekends. No holidays. No after-hours noise (drilling, hammering, sawing).

This affects your project timeline. A renovation that would take 4 weeks in a detached home often takes 6–8 weeks in a condo because of the compressed work day and the no-weekend rule.
